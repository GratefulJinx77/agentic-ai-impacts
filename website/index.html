Executive Summary

Five Things Everyone Should Know

The most important findings, simplified.

1

The Entry-Level Job Crisis Is Real

-13% employment for workers 22-25 in AI-exposed jobs

The most reliable statistic in the research. Entry-level postings down 35% since 2023. AI replaces what textbooks teach but can't replace experience — yet the pathway to gaining experience is disappearing.

Reliability: A (Very High)
2

The "171% ROI" Is a Fantasy Number

1.3% actual total productivity gain since ChatGPT

The widely-cited 171% ROI measures what executives hope to achieve, not actual returns. Only 10% of organizations see significant measurable ROI. Aggregate productivity gains? About 1.3% total.

171% ROI Reliability: D (Very Low)
3

AI Security Has an Unsolvable Vulnerability

35% of all 2025 AI security incidents from prompt injection

OpenAI admitted prompt injection "is unlikely to ever be fully solved." Any AI agent that reads emails or browses the web can be manipulated. The first AI-orchestrated cyberattack already happened.

Reliability: Established Consensus
4

Human Skills Are Eroding — Measurably

39pp gap between perceived vs. actual productivity

Doctors using AI see diagnostic accuracy drop when AI is removed. Developers feel 20% faster but are objectively 19% slower. The better the AI, the less humans practice. The less they practice, the more dependent they become.

Reliability: A- (Published Studies)
5

No One Wrote Rules for This

0 countries with agentic AI-specific legislation

Not a single country has enacted legislation specifically designed for autonomous AI agents. 118 of 193 countries have no participation in any significant AI governance initiative.

Reliability: A- (UN Data)

Ethics & Society

The Deeper Questions

Beyond economics and security, agentic AI raises fundamental questions about human agency, democracy, and justice.

Power Concentration

"No electorate voted to grant a handful of Silicon Valley companies the ability to deploy autonomous decision-making systems that affect billions of lives."

~100 companies account for >40% of global private AI investment. The top 5 tech firms control >80% of the agentic AI platform market. This is political power without democratic authorization.

The Comfort Trap

"The better AI works, the less humans practice. The less humans practice, the more dependent they become. The more dependent they become, the more catastrophic the consequences of AI failure."

Gartner projects that by 2030, half of enterprises will face irreversible skill shortages. The efficiency that makes AI valuable is the same property that erodes human capability.

Epistemic Collapse

"Not just believing false things, but losing confidence that we can know anything at all."

When genuine content is dismissed as "probably AI," the collapse is complete. Deepfake existence undermines trust in all media. Research shows people remain emotionally swayed by deepfakes even after being told they're fake.

The Autonomy Paradox

"People exercise their autonomy to delegate decision-making to AI, which then reduces their capacity for autonomous decision-making in the future."

Is choosing to delegate a legitimate exercise of autonomy, or a self-defeating one? The erosion is gradual, distributed, and non-obvious.

Informed Consent Is Structurally Impossible

When you tell an AI agent to "handle my email," you can't predict what actions it will take. The more autonomous the agent, the less meaningful any prior consent becomes. This isn't a design flaw — it's the fundamental tension between autonomy and control.

The Dignity of Labor

If AI makes many forms of human labor unnecessary, what happens to the connection between work and dignity? The question is not merely economic (can workers find new jobs?) but existential (what happens when the activities that defined identity are performed by machines?).

Cross-Agent Conflicts

Where the Experts Disagree

Our agents didn't always agree. We preserved disagreements as evidence — not bugs.

#1

Jobs: Net Positive or Structural Crisis?

+

Net Positive (WEF/Gemini)

170M created vs. 92M displaced = +78M net by 2030. New role categories emerging.

K-Shaped Divergence (Economist)

Net positive obscures distributional reality. New jobs need different skills, geographies, demographics. Entry-level pipeline permanently disrupted.

Beyond Augmentation/Replacement (Ethics)

0.87 correlation between automation and augmentation in same roles. The distinction is collapsing. The real question is about meaning, not jobs.

Data Scientist: Stanford/ADP data (Grade A) confirms real displacement now. WEF (Grade B+) projects aggregate future. "The same workers who lose jobs are NOT the same ones who get new roles."
#2

ROI: Extraordinary Returns or Measurement Artifact?

+

Strong Returns (Vendor Reports)

171% average ROI. 74% report some ROI within first year. 40-60% productivity gains.

The Paradox (Economist + Data Scientist)

171% is expected ROI, not measured. Only 10% see significant returns. Aggregate productivity: 1.3% total. Survivorship bias, sunk cost psychology, J-curve effects.

Data Scientist: PagerDuty survey methodology is solid (B), but as evidence of actual returns it's a D. "This is what executives hope to achieve, not what they have achieved."
#3

Inequality: Widening or Narrowing?

+

Widening (IMF)

AI investment raises Gini index. Productivity gains concentrated at higher incomes. Top 1% share at record 31.7%.

Narrowing (CEPR)

AI "substantially reduces wage inequality while raising average wages by 21%." Task simplification enables cross-skill competition.

Economist's reconciliation: Both are correct. AI within organizations compresses gaps. AI across the economy concentrates wealth. The net effect depends on diffusion speed. Currently, the concentration force is winning.
#4

Historical Analogies: Instructive or Misleading?

+

Informative (Economist)

Electricity took 30+ years. Computers took 10+. AI follows GPT patterns: J-curve, complementary investment, lag.

Qualitatively Different (Ethics)

Previous disruptions targeted manual/routine cognitive labor. AI targets non-routine cognitive labor — the jobs previous disruptions created. No guaranteed "upward" displacement pathway.

Assessment: Not fully contradictory. Economist uses history for adoption dynamics. Ethics argues the framework breaks for cognitive labor. The disagreement is about whether patterns are predictive or merely illustrative.
#5

How Fast to Regulate?

+

Rapid Regulation Needed (Governance, Ethics)

Governance vacuum is actively harmful. Agents deploying at production scale with no agentic-specific law. "A society that cannot govern a technology that reshapes its fundamental institutions is not self-governing."

Innovation-First (US Federal Policy)

"Minimally burdensome" approach. Premature regulation stifles innovation. Safe harbors and cure periods.

Assessment: This is a values question, not a factual one. It depends on which failure mode you consider more dangerous: under-regulation or over-regulation. No amount of data resolves this.

Meta-Observation: Why Disagreements Are Evidence

  • Aggregate vs. distributional: Most disagreements dissolve when you see both views can be simultaneously correct
  • Measurement vs. expectation: The most optimistic numbers tend to have the weakest methodology
  • Values, not just facts: The deepest disagreements are about what matters, not what's true
  • Vendor bias gradient: Sources closer to the AI industry produce more optimistic numbers. Systematic, not random.
